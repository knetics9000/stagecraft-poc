# Partner Meeting Agenda — Kurt × Faisal

**Project:** Stagecraft (working name)
**Purpose:** The decision meeting. Walk Faisal through the competitive landscape and the probability assessments together, work through the five critical decisions one at a time, and reach a documented agreement to either proceed under specific terms, pivot meaningfully, or walk away.
**Estimated time:** 90 minutes minimum. Don't try to do this in 30 — it's the most important conversation of the project.
**Outcome:** A signed (or at least written and dated) partnership memo capturing the decisions made, OR a clean conclusion that the project doesn't proceed.

---

## Before the Meeting

**What to send Faisal 48 hours in advance:**

1. `POC_Spec_AI_Stage_Classifier.md`
2. `Competitive_Analysis_Day_AI.md`
3. `Competitive_Analysis_Coffee_AI.md`
4. `Mirofish_Simulation_Results.md`

Send them with a short note. Suggested wording:

> "Hey Faisal — before we meet, I want you to read these four documents. They cover what we'd be building, who we'd be competing against, and a couple of independent analyses on how likely this project is to succeed. The competitive picture is more crowded than either of us expected, and the probability of building this into a real business is lower than we'd hope. I want us both to look at the same facts before we decide whether to do this and how. None of it is meant to discourage you — it's meant to make sure we go in with our eyes open. Read them, sit with them, and let's meet [date/time]."

**Why send in advance:** Faisal needs time to process the Day.ai $24M Sequoia / Coffee AI / 6% probability findings privately. If he sees them for the first time in the meeting, he'll be reacting emotionally instead of thinking strategically. Give him 48 hours to land.

**What to bring to the meeting:**

- Printed copies of all four documents (some people read paper differently than screens)
- This agenda (also printed)
- A blank Google Doc or notepad where decisions get recorded in real time
- The mirofish setup, in case he wants to see another scenario simulated live

## The Meeting

### Opening (5 minutes)

Set the frame explicitly. Suggested opener:

> "Faisal, I want to be useful to you on this project, and the way I can be most useful is to be honest with you about what I've learned. I've spent the week researching the space, building out a POC plan, analyzing the competition, and running independent simulations on the probability of this working. Some of what I found is encouraging. A lot of it is hard. I want to walk you through it together, and then I want us to make a real decision — not a polite one — about whether and how we do this. Sound good?"

Wait for his actual response. If he says "let's go" without engaging with the framing, gently re-anchor: "Before we start, I want to make sure you and I are both ready to actually decide today — not just talk."

### Part 1 — The Competitive Picture (15 minutes)

Walk him through Day.ai and Coffee AI. He's read the docs by now, so don't re-read them — discuss them.

**What to surface:**

- Day.ai exists, has $24M from Sequoia, is run by the ex-CPO of HubSpot, and is doing approximately what we'd be doing — but using meeting recordings as input.
- Coffee AI exists, has Salesforce Ventures backing, sells flat-fee transparent pricing, dominates SEO for "best AI sales tool" queries.
- The wedge that differentiates Stagecraft (rep narrative input + vertical focus + management-layer-first) is real and structurally beats both at once — that's an unusually strong signal.
- But we are entering a market two well-funded competitors already inhabit. We will not outspend them. We will not out-engineer them on velocity. The only way we win is with sharper focus on customers they can't serve well.

**Question to put to Faisal directly:**

> "Knowing this competitive picture, do you still believe there's a project here? And if yes — what's the one-sentence reason you believe a customer would pick us over Day.ai?"

Listen carefully. If he can answer that sentence cleanly, the project has a real foundation. If he can't, the project doesn't yet have a real differentiator, and that's the work before anything else.

### Part 2 — The Probability Picture (10 minutes)

Walk him through the convergence: Claude and Mirofish independently both said ~6–10% probability of $1M ARR in 24 months, but both said it rises to 15–20% if five specific decisions are executed well.

**What to emphasize:**

- This is not a forecast that the project will fail. It is the honest base rate for what we're attempting.
- Most startups don't succeed. That's normal.
- The actionable insight is the gap between 6% and 20% — that gap is execution discipline, not luck.
- The 6% number isn't a verdict. It's a starting point. If we don't like the number, we change it by changing what we do.

**Question to put to Faisal:**

> "Knowing this is — at best — a 1-in-5 shot even with great execution, do you want to do this? Be honest. There's no wrong answer. Some people thrive on those odds and some people need higher odds to commit. Both are valid."

His answer here matters enormously. If he hedges or says "well, we'll just have to execute well" without engaging with the probability — push back. The point is for him to actually internalize the odds, not nod past them.

### Part 3 — The Five Decisions (45 minutes)

This is the heart of the meeting. Work through each decision, one at a time. Don't skip. Write down the agreement on each.

**Decision 1 — Partnership terms in writing (Month 0)**

Discuss:
- Equity split. What does each of you get for what contribution?
- Time commitment. How many hours/week, full-time vs. part-time, when does that change?
- Decision rights. Who has final say on product, distribution, hiring, fundraising?
- Vesting. What happens if one of you leaves at 6 months? 12 months?
- Exit clause. What's the explicit way to wind this down if it isn't working?

**Get to a written summary by end of meeting.** Doesn't need to be a lawyered agreement (that comes next, with a startup lawyer who charges $1–2K for a founder's agreement) — but the terms need to be specific and both of you need to sign your name to the summary in this meeting.

**Decision 2 — Vertical focus (Month 1)**

Discuss:
- Faisal's three candidate verticals are EdTech, biotech, and recruiting. Which does he know coldest? Which has the strongest network?
- The Mirofish simulation specifically recommends biotech BD (regulated industry where Day.ai's recording dependence is structurally disabled). Discuss whether that argument holds for Faisal.
- Whatever you choose, commit to it for at least 12 months before reconsidering. Vertical pivots are expensive.

**Get to a single vertical by end of meeting.** Write it down: "Stagecraft will initially focus on [VERTICAL]. We will not pursue other verticals until [milestone] is achieved."

**Decision 3 — Full-time commitment (Month 2)**

Discuss:
- Faisal: when does he go full-time? What does "full-time" mean financially? Does he have 6–12 months of runway personally to live without a paycheck?
- Kurt: how many hours/week can you actually commit, sustainably, without burning out? Is this nights-and-weekends, or are you carving real time?
- If Faisal can't go full-time within 2–3 months, the project's probability drops significantly. Acknowledge that.

**Get to specific hour commitments and trigger dates.** "Faisal goes full-time by [date] OR when [condition]." "Kurt commits X hours/week from [date]."

**Decision 4 — Distribution strategy with measurable validation (Month 3)**

Discuss:
- Faisal's network of sales leaders is the proposed distribution channel. List the actual names — how many real people will agree to take a POC demo call in the first 60 days?
- Set a hard checkpoint: by Month 5, X of those contacts must convert to paid pilots (even $1 pilots — the commitment matters more than the dollars). Below that number, the distribution thesis is invalidated and the project needs to either find another channel or wind down.

**Get to a named list and a numeric checkpoint.** "Faisal will demo to [10+ named people] by [date]. If fewer than [X] convert to pilots by [date], we re-evaluate the project."

**Decision 5 — POC scope and "Why us" positioning (Month 5)**

Discuss:
- The POC stays strictly limited to the AI stage classifier. No scope creep into pipeline views, dashboards, integrations, mobile, multi-tenant, or anything else.
- The "Why us, not Day.ai?" sentence has to exist before any demos happen. Write it together in the meeting if possible: "Stagecraft is for ___ who need ___, and unlike Day.ai we ___."

**Get the sentence written.** If you can't write it in the meeting, that's the most important homework Faisal has between now and the next checkpoint.

### Part 4 — Kurt's Own Commitment (10 minutes)

Be honest with Faisal about what you can and can't bring.

**What to say plainly:**

- You're technically literate but not a traditional engineer. You'll build the POC using AI coding assistants. You're confident you can do it, but iteration speed will be slower than a senior engineer.
- You can commit [specific hours/week] sustainably. More than that becomes unsustainable.
- You'll own technical decisions and architecture; he owns product positioning, sales strategy, and domain logic.
- If the project gets traction and needs a real engineering team, you will help recruit a founding engineer — and that may eventually mean diluting both of you, or you stepping back into a different role.

**Question to put to Faisal:**

> "Given what I can bring and what I can't — is this enough? Or do you need a co-founder with deeper engineering background, and you'd rather have me as an advisor instead of a partner?"

Let him answer honestly. Either answer is fine. Pretending you can do something you can't is not fine.

### Part 5 — The Decision (5 minutes)

By this point in the meeting, the decision should be obvious to both of you. Make it explicit.

**One of three outcomes:**

- **Proceed under the terms we've documented.** Both sign the meeting memo. Set the next milestone date (probably 30 days out for the first checkpoint: vertical confirmed, partnership agreement drafted with a lawyer, customer interviews scheduled).
- **Pivot meaningfully.** Identify what specifically needs to change before you'd say yes — different vertical, different role for Kurt, raise capital first, etc. Set a date to revisit.
- **Walk away.** This is a real, valid, honorable outcome. If neither of you can fully commit to all five decisions, the project doesn't have a strategy yet, and starting anyway is worse than not starting.

Whichever it is — write it down, both sign and date it, and end the meeting.

## After the Meeting

**Within 48 hours:**

- Send Faisal a clean written summary of the decisions made (or the decision to walk away)
- If proceeding: schedule the first 30-day checkpoint
- If proceeding: each of you executes your first action item
  - Faisal: book first 5 customer-validation calls
  - Kurt: start the prompt-engineering scratchpad on the AI stage classifier
- If proceeding and within budget: schedule the lawyer call for the founder's agreement

**Re-run mirofish quarterly.** Update the input documents with whatever you've learned and re-simulate. The probability number should move. If it doesn't, something is wrong.

## Difficult Conversations You Might Have to Have

A few specific tough moments to prepare for:

**If Faisal says "Day.ai doesn't matter, our wedge is different":**

> "I want to believe that too. But Day.ai's product manager has read everything we've read, and the founder built HubSpot's CRM. If our wedge is real, they will copy it within 6 months once they see us validate it. We have to be ahead of that — which means specific vertical depth they can't replicate."

**If Faisal pushes for higher probability ("I think this is more like 30%"):**

> "Maybe. But two independent analyses on the same facts said 6–10%. I'd rather plan for the realistic case and be pleasantly surprised than plan for the optimistic case and run out of runway. We can come back to the probability question after our first 10 customer conversations — those will move the number more than any analysis."

**If Faisal can't commit to full-time within 3 months:**

> "That's not a deal-breaker, but it changes what we're building. A part-time founder builds a different project than a full-time founder. We'd need to lower the ambition, slow the timeline, or find someone else to be the full-time sales/founder voice. Which of those are you open to?"

**If Faisal won't sign written partnership terms:**

> "I understand it feels formal between friends. But every co-founder dispute I've seen starts with 'we didn't think we needed to write it down.' I'd rather have an awkward 30 minutes now than a project-ending conflict in month 6. This is for both of us."

**If the answer becomes "we should walk away":**

> "I'm glad we figured this out before we spent six months on it. The idea is real, the analysis is honest, and we both know what we'd need to make it work. If those conditions change later, we can come back to it. For now, let's call it cleanly and stay friends."

---

## What Success Looks Like for This Meeting

The meeting is a success if:

1. Both of you walk out with the same understanding of the competitive landscape and the probability picture
2. Either you have a documented commitment to proceed under specific terms, OR you have a clean decision not to
3. Whatever you decide, you decided it together, with honest facts, and in writing
4. The friendship is intact

A meeting that produces "we'll think about it and talk again next week" is a meeting that failed. Don't leave without a decision.
